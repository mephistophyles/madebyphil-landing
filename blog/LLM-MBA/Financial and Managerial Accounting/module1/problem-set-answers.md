Solutions for Problem 1:

Three key differences between financial and managerial reports:

Regulation and Format: Financial statements follow strict GAAP/IFRS requirements with standardized formats, while managerial reports are customized for internal needs. This difference exists because external reports need comparability across companies, while internal reports need to address specific management questions.
Level of Detail: Financial statements aggregate information at the entity level, while managerial reports provide breakdowns by product line and region. This difference exists because investors need a comprehensive view of the entire company, while managers need granular information to make operational decisions.
Time Orientation: Financial statements report historical performance, while managerial reports likely include forward-looking projections and variance analyses. This difference exists because financial reporting requirements focus on verifiable past performance, while management needs predictive information for planning.


Use of each type of report in CEO decision-making:

Financial Reports: Monitor overall financial health, evaluate performance against market expectations, communicate results to the board and investors, ensure regulatory compliance, and understand high-level trends affecting the company as a whole.
Managerial Reports: Identify underperforming product lines or regions, allocate resources effectively, evaluate executive performance, make pricing decisions, plan strategic initiatives, and target cost-cutting efforts.


Limitations of each report type:

Financial Reports: Historical focus limits predictive value, aggregation obscures segment performance, standardized metrics may not capture industry-specific value drivers, and compliance focus may prioritize form over substance.
Managerial Reports: Lack of standardization may hinder comparability across divisions, customization may reflect biases of report creators, unaudited data may contain errors, and complexity may obscure key insights if poorly designed.

Solutions for Problem 3:

Key regulatory bodies and their roles:

Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC):

Primary regulator of public companies in the U.S.
Reviews and comments on registration statements and periodic filings
Enforces securities laws and can investigate potential violations
Requires extensive disclosures beyond basic financial statements


Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB):

Establishes GAAP standards that public companies must follow
Issues Accounting Standards Updates (ASUs) that evolve reporting requirements
Provides implementation guidance on complex accounting issues


Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB):

Oversees the audits of public companies
Establishes audit standards
Inspects registered accounting firms
Influences the depth and scope of external audits


Stock Exchange (NYSE or NASDAQ):

Establishes listing requirements including governance standards
Requires timely disclosure of material information
Can delist companies for non-compliance
